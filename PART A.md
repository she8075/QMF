## Deliverable 2 — Monetary Sovereignty and Implications for the Counterfactual

The chronology of Ukraine's exchange rate regimes reveals that the NBU's effective monetary sovereignty was near-absent before 2016 and abruptly suspended in 2022. From 2000 to April 2008, Ukraine maintained a *de facto* dollar peg (*conventional pegged arrangement* in AREAER terms), despite a *de jure* declaration of managed floating since February 2000 with no operational change, what the IMF itself describes as a case of *fear of floating* (IMF Selected Issues, 2016; Calvo & Reinhart, 2002). The EFF Request (IMF Country Report No. 15/69) confirms retrospectively that "periods of more exchange rate flexibility were forced and short lived" throughout this era. During this prolonged dollar-peg phase, euro area membership would have essentially **substituted one nominal anchor for another**, the dollar for the euro. This substitution is not neutral, however: the EUR/USD rate fluctuated significantly over the period (notably a marked euro appreciation in 2003–2007), so that a euro anchor would have imported a different level and profile of imported inflation, even if the regime change in terms of macromonetary credibility would have been limited.

The devaluation episodes of 2008–2009 and 2014–2015 did not represent the deliberate exercise of monetary sovereignty: in both cases, they resulted from reserve depletion and speculative pressure that the NBU could not contain, with the rate re-fixed as soon as conditions allowed. Both episodes were accompanied by significant capital controls. In 2008–2009, emergency restrictions on FX purchases and bank deposit withdrawals were imposed in October 2008 and partially maintained through May 2010. In 2014–2015, the NBU introduced a 75% mandatory export surrender requirement, caps on individual FX purchases and a ban on dividend repatriation from OTC securities (EFF Request No. 15/69), while the policy rate was raised to 30% in February 2015. The EPE 2016 (No. 16/320) records a cumulative depreciation of approximately 90% in 2014 relative to end-2013. The EFF Request projected inflation at ~27% for 2015; the actual outturn was 48% (wiiw, 2017). These trajectories, large devaluations followed by inflationary absorption, are structurally absent under a euro area membership scenario: the adjustment would instead have required internal devaluation (compression of wages and prices), as experienced by Greece, Ireland and the Baltic states during 2010–2012.

The **first and only period of genuine discretionary monetary sovereignty** runs from January 2016 to February 2022. The NBU operated a genuine IT regime with an explicit target path (12% in 2016, 8% in 2017, 6% in 2018, 5% ±1pp from 2019) and the policy rate as the primary instrument. Whether the NBU's behaviour actually changed in practice is nuanced: the policy rate was actively deployed and capital controls were gradually lifted but credibility remained fragile, inflation expectations persistently exceeded the 5% target (IMF 2016) and wiiw (2017) argues that macroeconomic stabilisation in 2016–2018 owed more to the gradual normalisation of capital flows than to IT itself. This is the only period where the NBU exercised genuine discretion that euro area membership would have replaced with ECB monetary policy.

This chronology has a direct implication for the counterfactual identification strategy: euro area membership would have represented a treatment of *time-varying intensity*. During dollar-peg phases, treatment would have been partial, an anchor substitution but not the suppression of effective discretion. During forced devaluation episodes, the counterfactual treatment is maximum in terms of inflationary impact but not in terms of lost sovereignty (which was already absent). Full treatment intensity is concentrated in 2016–2022. The post-October 2023 period represents an intermediate and increasing treatment intensity: the wartime "flexible IT" is not full sovereignty but a growing autonomy still constrained by the IMF's Extended Fund Facility conditionality.
